Why I Deleted a 0.996 Correlated Feature — And Why That Was Right

Preeti Bhardwaj  ·  May 2026

When I was building a bank customer churn prediction model on 10,000 records, my correlation matrix threw up something that made me stop and stare.

One feature — Complain (whether a customer had filed a complaint) — had a 0.996 correlation with the target variable (Exited). That's almost perfect. A data scientist's dream, right?

I deleted it anyway. Here's why.

What the number was telling me

A 0.996 correlation means: almost every customer who complained, churned. Almost every customer who didn't complain, stayed. On the surface, that sounds like a powerful signal. Your model would love it.

But ask yourself — in the real world, when does a bank know a customer has complained?

Only after the customer has already complained. Which happens right before they leave. So what you've built is not a churn predictor. It's a churn confirmer. By the time you have the Complain value, the customer has one foot out the door. There's no time to intervene.

The difference between correlation and usefulness

Test 1 — Is it statistically useful? (correlation, feature importance)
Test 2 — Is it available and actionable at prediction time?

Complain passed Test 1 with flying colours. It failed Test 2 completely.

What I kept instead

After removing Complain, SHAP analysis revealed the actual drivers: Age (older customers churn more), NumOfProducts (single-product customers churn at much higher rates), and Geography (Germany showed 32.4% churn vs ~16% in France and Spain).

These are features a retention team can actually use. You can identify a 45-year-old single-product German customer today and reach out before they decide to leave.

The model still performed well

ROC-AUC: 0.847 · Mean CV AUC: 0.87 across 5 folds

A lower AUC built on honest features is worth more than a higher AUC built on a feature that tells you what you already know.

Building Your First RAG Pipeline — No Fluff, Just Steps

Preeti Bhardwaj  ·  May 2026

This is the tutorial I wish I had when I built the Medical RAG Assistant — no fluff, no jargon walls, just the actual steps to build a working RAG pipeline from scratch.

What is RAG and why does it matter?

RAG = Retrieval-Augmented Generation. LLMs don't know your documents. RAG fixes this: it takes your documents, turns them into searchable chunks, finds the most relevant ones for your question, and sends them to the LLM as context. The LLM answers from your documents, not from training data — and can cite the source.

Stack

Python · LangChain · FAISS · Groq (free API, no credit card) · MiniLM embeddings (local, free)

Step 1 — Install

pip install langchain langchain-groq faiss-cpu pypdf sentence-transformers langchain-community

Step 2 — Load your PDF

from langchain_community.document_loaders import PyPDFLoader
loader = PyPDFLoader("your_document.pdf")
documents = loader.load()

Step 3 — Chunk it

from langchain_text_splitters import RecursiveCharacterTextSplitter
splitter = RecursiveCharacterTextSplitter(chunk_size=600, chunk_overlap=80)
chunks = splitter.split_documents(documents)

Step 4 — Embed and store

from langchain_community.embeddings import HuggingFaceEmbeddings
from langchain_community.vectorstores import FAISS
embeddings = HuggingFaceEmbeddings(model_name="sentence-transformers/all-MiniLM-L6-v2")
vectorstore = FAISS.from_documents(chunks, embeddings)

Step 5 — Connect Groq and ask

from langchain_groq import ChatGroq
llm = ChatGroq(model="llama-3.1-8b-instant", temperature=0.1, api_key="your-gsk-key")
retriever = vectorstore.as_retriever(search_kwargs={"k": 5})
# Now build your chain and ask questions!

The hallucination-aware trick

The most important part is your prompt. Tell the model explicitly: "If the answer is not in the context, say you cannot find it. Never make up medical facts." This is what separates a toy RAG from a trustworthy one.

SHAP Values Explained Simply — Without Math Jargon

Preeti Bhardwaj  ·  June 2026

Every ML tutorial shows you how to train a model. Almost none of them tell you why the model made the decision it did. That's where SHAP comes in — and it's one of the most practically useful tools I've added to my workflow.

The problem SHAP solves

Imagine you build a PCOS detection model with 0.97 AUC. Your model is accurate. But your doctor asks: "Why did you flag this patient as high risk?" You can't say "because the model said so." That's not medicine. That's a black box.

SHAP — SHapley Additive exPlanations — opens the black box. It tells you, for every single prediction, how much each feature pushed the output up or down.

The core idea (no equations)

Think of your model's prediction as a starting point — the average prediction across all patients. SHAP then asks: for this specific patient, which features moved the prediction away from that average, and by how much?

SHAP value = how much did this feature contribute to this prediction, compared to the average?

A positive SHAP value means the feature pushed the prediction higher (more likely PCOS). A negative value means it pushed it lower. The magnitude tells you how strongly.

What I found in the PCOS project

When I ran SHAP on the XGBoost classifier trained on 541 patient records, the results were medically interesting:

  • Total follicle count — the single strongest predictor. High follicle count = strong push toward positive PCOS diagnosis. Makes clinical sense.
  • Fast food consumption — appeared as a top-3 risk factor. Diet and PCOS have a documented link, and SHAP surfaced it from the data alone.
  • BMI and weight gain — significant contributors, consistent with clinical literature.

These aren't just feature importances from a leaderboard. These are decision-level explanations that a doctor can look at and say "yes, this makes sense" — or flag if something looks wrong.

Three ways to use SHAP

1. Summary plot — shows global importance across all predictions. Which features matter most overall?

import shap
explainer = shap.TreeExplainer(model)
shap_values = explainer.shap_values(X_test)
shap.summary_plot(shap_values, X_test)

2. Beeswarm plot — my favourite. Shows both importance AND direction. Each dot is one prediction. Red = high feature value, blue = low. The x-axis shows SHAP impact.

shap.plots.beeswarm(shap.Explanation(
    values=shap_values,
    base_values=explainer.expected_value,
    data=X_test,
    feature_names=X_test.columns.tolist()
))

3. Force plot (single prediction) — explains one patient's result. Red arrows pushed the prediction up, blue arrows pushed it down.

shap.force_plot(explainer.expected_value, shap_values[0], X_test.iloc[0])

The thing most tutorials miss

SHAP works best on TreeExplainer for tree-based models (XGBoost, Random Forest, LightGBM). It's exact and fast. For neural networks or linear models, use DeepExplainer or LinearExplainer — but the interpretation is the same.

The bigger lesson: explainability isn't a nice-to-have. In healthcare, finance, or any domain where a decision affects a real person, being able to explain why your model made a call is not optional. SHAP makes that possible.

SMOTE Inside a Pipeline — Why Placement Matters for Data Leakage

Preeti Bhardwaj  ·  June 2026

Most SMOTE tutorials look like this:

from imblearn.over_sampling import SMOTE
sm = SMOTE()
X_resampled, y_resampled = sm.fit_resample(X, y)

# Then split...
X_train, X_test, y_train, y_test = train_test_split(X_resampled, y_resampled)

This is wrong. And the model you build with it is lying to you.

What SMOTE actually does

SMOTE — Synthetic Minority Over-sampling Technique — creates synthetic samples of the minority class by interpolating between existing minority samples. If you have 100 PCOS-positive patients and 400 negative, SMOTE generates new synthetic positive patients to balance the dataset.

The goal is to stop your model from just predicting "negative" for everything and still being 80% accurate. Imbalanced data is a real problem. SMOTE is a real solution. But only if you use it correctly.

Why the common approach leaks data

When you apply SMOTE to the entire dataset before splitting, synthetic samples built from your training data end up in your test set. Your cross-validation is no longer testing on unseen data — it's testing on data it effectively already "saw" when SMOTE interpolated from it.

Your AUC goes up. Your model looks great. And then it fails in production, because real-world data wasn't generated by SMOTE.

Rule: anything that learns from data — including SMOTE — must only see training data, never test data.

The fix: SMOTE inside ImbPipeline

ImbPipeline from imbalanced-learn is the correct tool. It applies SMOTE only inside each cross-validation fold — on the training portion — and leaves the validation fold untouched.

from imblearn.pipeline import Pipeline as ImbPipeline
from imblearn.over_sampling import SMOTE
from sklearn.preprocessing import StandardScaler
from xgboost import XGBClassifier
from sklearn.model_selection import StratifiedKFold, cross_val_score

pipeline = ImbPipeline([
    ('scaler', StandardScaler()),
    ('smote', SMOTE(random_state=42)),
    ('model', XGBClassifier(random_state=42))
])

cv = StratifiedKFold(n_splits=5, shuffle=True, random_state=42)
scores = cross_val_score(pipeline, X, y, cv=cv, scoring='roc_auc')
print(f"Mean AUC: {scores.mean():.3f}")

With this setup, for each of the 5 folds: SMOTE runs on the 80% training portion only, the model trains on the balanced training data, and evaluation happens on the 20% that SMOTE never touched. That's a honest AUC.

Why StratifiedKFold matters too

Regular KFold splits randomly. With imbalanced data, you can end up with a fold that has almost no minority class samples. StratifiedKFold preserves the class ratio in every fold — so each validation set is representative of the real distribution.

The result in the PCOS project

Using ImbPipeline with SMOTE inside StratifiedKFold on 541 patient records gave a Mean ROC-AUC of 0.97 across 5 folds with 90%+ recall on positive cases. That's a result I trust — because the evaluation was honest.

When someone asks me "how do you know this will generalise?" I can point to the pipeline and show them exactly why leakage was impossible.

From Notebook to Endpoint — Taking a Model Past "It Works On My Machine"

Preeti Bhardwaj  ·  June 2026

Most ML portfolios stop at a notebook. You train a model, get a good AUC, plot a confusion matrix, and call it done. That's a real skill — but it's not the same skill as putting a model somewhere another piece of software can actually call it. The Hospital Readmission Predictor was my attempt to close that gap, end to end.

The question that started it

A trained model sitting in a .pkl file isn't useful to anyone except the person who trained it. A hospital's existing software doesn't know how to "open a notebook." It needs something it can send patient data to and get a risk score back — instantly, reliably, the same way every time. That's the gap between a model and a product.

Step 1 — Wrapping the model in an API

I used FastAPI to expose the trained XGBoost model as an HTTP endpoint. The pattern is simple but it matters: incoming data gets validated with pydantic schemas before it ever reaches the model, so malformed input fails loudly with a clear error instead of silently breaking a prediction.

from fastapi import FastAPI
from pydantic import BaseModel
import joblib

app = FastAPI()
model = joblib.load("model.pkl")

class PatientData(BaseModel):
    age: int
    num_procedures: int
    num_diagnoses: int
    # ...remaining features

@app.post("/predict")
def predict(data: PatientData):
    features = [[data.age, data.num_procedures, data.num_diagnoses]]
    risk_score = model.predict_proba(features)[0][1]
    return {"readmission_risk": float(risk_score)}

That's the whole contract: send patient features, get a probability back. Anything downstream — a dashboard, an internal hospital tool, a scheduling system — can now talk to the model without knowing it's XGBoost underneath.

Step 2 — Why Docker, not just "pip install"

"It works on my machine" is the most expensive sentence in software. My laptop has a specific Python version, specific package versions, specific OS quirks. None of that is guaranteed to exist wherever this actually needs to run.

Docker solves this by packaging the exact environment — Python version, dependencies, the app itself — into one image that runs identically anywhere a container can run.

FROM python:3.11-slim
WORKDIR /app
COPY requirements.txt .
RUN pip install --no-cache-dir -r requirements.txt
COPY . .
EXPOSE 8000
CMD ["uvicorn", "app.main:app", "--host", "0.0.0.0", "--port", "8000"]

Once this builds, the question "does it work on your machine?" stops being relevant. It works inside the container, and the container is the same everywhere.

Step 3 — CI/CD: catching breakage before it ships

The last piece is GitHub Actions. Every time code is pushed, a workflow automatically spins up, installs dependencies, and runs the test suite against the FastAPI app. If a change breaks the /predict endpoint or the model loading logic, the pipeline fails immediately — before it ever reaches anything resembling production.

The point of CI/CD isn't speed. It's that a human doesn't have to remember to test things — the pipeline remembers for you, every single time.

This is the same underlying principle behind using ImbPipeline with SMOTE in my PCOS project: don't trust a manual step that's easy to forget. Encode the correct behavior into the system itself, so it happens automatically whether you remember to do it or not.

Why this matters more than another 0.01 AUC

It's tempting to chase model accuracy as the main metric of a project. But a model with excellent AUC that can't be reliably served, breaks silently on bad input, or can't be redeployed without manual babysitting isn't actually usable in a real setting. A slightly simpler model wrapped in a tested, containerized, automatically-verified pipeline is worth more in practice — because it's the version that can actually be trusted to run unattended.

That's the real lesson from building this: in healthcare especially, "did we test this properly before it touched real data" is not optional, and it shouldn't depend on someone remembering to do it by hand.
